Mass-Spring System in 3D

Mass-Spring System in 3D

Configuration

  • $ \mathbf q = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf x_1 \\ \mathbf x_2 \end{pmatrix} $
  • $ \mathbf {\dot q} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf {\dot x}_1 \\ \mathbf {\dot x}_2 \end{pmatrix} $

Kinetic Energy:

$$T = \frac 1 2 \mathbf {\dot q} ^ T \mathbf M \mathbf {\dot q} $$

where $ \mathbf M = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf M_0 && 0 \\ 0 && \mathbf M_1 \\ \end{pmatrix} $

Potential Energy:

$$V = \frac 1 2 k (l - r)^2 $$

where

$$l = \norm {\begin{pmatrix} -I && I \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf x_1 \\ \mathbf x_2 \end{pmatrix} } = \sqrt {\mathbf {\Delta x}^T \mathbf {\Delta x} } = \sqrt {\mathbf q^T \mathbf B^T \mathbf B \mathbf q } $$

The Lagrangian

$$L = \frac 1 2 \mathbf {\dot q} ^ T \mathbf M \mathbf {\dot q} - \frac 1 2 k (\sqrt {\mathbf q^T \mathbf B^T \mathbf B \mathbf q } - r)^2 $$

Euler-Lagrange Equation

notice that only $ V $ is concerned with $ \mathbf q $, the left side is actually the force.

$$\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{\dot{q}}} = - \frac{\partial V}{\partial \mathbf{q}} $$

so, there is

$$\mathbf M \mathbf {\ddot q} = - \frac{\partial V}{\partial \mathbf{q}} $$

Large Mass-Spring System in 3D

Configuration

  • $ \mathbf q = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf x_1 \\ ... \\ \mathbf x_n \end{pmatrix} $
  • $ \mathbf {\dot q} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf {\dot x}_1 \\ ... \\ \mathbf {\dot x}_n \end{pmatrix} $

Kinetic Energy:

$$T = \frac 1 2 \mathbf {\dot q} ^ T \mathbf M \mathbf {\dot q} $$

where $ \mathbf M = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf M_0 && \cdots && 0 \\ \vdots && \ddots && \vdots \\ 0 && \cdots && \mathbf M_n \end{pmatrix} \in \mathbb R^{3n \times 3n}$

Potential Energy:

$$V = \sum_{j=0}^{m-1}V_j(\mathbf x_A , \mathbf x_B) = \sum_{j=0}^{m-1}V_j(\mathbf E_j \mathbf q) $$

The Lagrangian

$$L = \frac 1 2 \mathbf {\dot q} ^ T \mathbf M \mathbf {\dot q} - \frac 1 2 k (\sqrt {\mathbf q^T \mathbf B^T \mathbf B \mathbf q } - r)^2 $$

Euler-Lagrange Equation

$$\mathbf M \mathbf {\ddot q} = - \frac{\partial V}{\partial \mathbf{q}} \\ - \frac{\partial V}{\partial \mathbf q } = - \frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{q}} \sum_{j=0}^{m-1}V_j(\mathbf E_j \mathbf q) = - \sum_{j=0}^{m-1} \frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf q } V_j(\mathbf E_j \mathbf q) = - \sum_{j=0}^{m-1} \mathbf E_j^T \frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf q_j} V_j (\mathbf q_j) = \sum_{j=0}^{m-1} \mathbf E_j^T \mathbf f_j (\mathbf q_j) $$

therefore

$$\mathbf M \mathbf {\ddot q} = \sum_{j=0}^{m-1} \mathbf E_j^T \mathbf f_j (\mathbf q_j) $$

Linearly-Implicit Time Integration

$$\mathbf M \mathbf {\dot q}^{t+1} = \mathbf M \mathbf {\dot q}^t + \Delta t \mathbf f( \mathbf q^{t+1}) \\ \mathbf q^{t+1} = \mathbf q^t + \Delta t \mathbf {\dot q}^{t+1} \\ $$

do approximation

$$\mathbf M \mathbf {\dot q}^{t+1} = \mathbf M \mathbf {\dot q}^t + \Delta t \mathbf f( \mathbf q^{t+1}) = \mathbf M \mathbf {\dot q}^t + \Delta t \mathbf f( \mathbf q^t + \Delta t \mathbf {\dot q}^{t+1}) = \mathbf M \mathbf {\dot q}^t + \Delta t \mathbf f( \mathbf q^t) + \Delta t^2 \frac {\partial {\mathbf f} } {\partial {\mathbf q}} \mathbf {\dot q}^{t+1} \\ \mathbf K = \frac {\partial {\mathbf f} } {\partial {\mathbf q}} = \sum_{j=0}^{m-1} [\mathbf E_j^T (-\frac {\partial^2 V_j} {\partial \mathbf q_j^2}) \mathbf E_j] \\ (\mathbf M - \Delta t^2 \mathbf K) \mathbf {\dot q}^{t+1} = \mathbf M \mathbf {\dot q}^t + \Delta t \mathbf f( \mathbf q^t) $$

with Fixed Boundary Conditions

CSC417-Lecture-02

Time Integration

How do we solve?

$$m \mathbf {\ddot q} = - k \mathbf q $$

More generally

$$m \mathbf {\ddot q} = f(\mathbf q, \mathbf {\dot q}) $$

The Coupled First Order System

considering the equation

$$m \mathbf {\ddot q} = - k \mathbf q $$

composing $ \mathbf y = {\begin{pmatrix} \mathbf {\dot q} \\ \mathbf {q} \end{pmatrix}}$, we may get

$$\begin{pmatrix} m & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \frac d {dt} \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf {\dot q} \\ \mathbf q \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -k \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf {\dot q} \\ \mathbf q \end{pmatrix} $$

which can be noted as a first-order ODE, aka. Coupled First Order System

$$\mathbf {A \dot y} = \mathbf { f(y) } \tag{1} $$

Numerical Integration

what we concern

  • Performance
  • Stability
  • Accuracy

consider

$$\mathbf {y^{t+1}} - \mathbf {y^t} = \mathbf A ^ {-1} \int _t ^{t+1} \mathbf f(\mathbf y) dt $$

Finite Difference

$$\mathbf {\dot y} \approx \frac 1 {\Delta t} (\mathbf {y^{t+1}} - \mathbf {y^t}) $$

we may get

$$\mathbf {y^{t+1}} - \mathbf {y^t} = \Delta t \mathbf A ^ {-1} \mathbf f( \mathbf y^t ) $$

aka.

$$\begin{array}{rl} \mathbf {\dot q}^{t+1} &=& \mathbf {\dot q}^t - \Delta t \frac k m \mathbf q^t \\ \mathbf {q}^{t+1} &=& \mathbf q^t + \Delta t \mathbf { \dot q} ^t \\ \end{array} $$

Runge-Kutta

$$\mathbf {y^{t+1}} - \mathbf {y^t} = \Delta t \mathbf A ^ {-1} ( \alpha \mathbf f( \mathbf y^{t+a} ) + \beta \mathbf f( \mathbf y^{t+b} ) ) $$

with $ a = 0, b = 1, \alpha = \beta = \frac 1 2 $, it becomes Heun’s Method

$$\mathbf {y^{t+1}} - \mathbf {y^t} = \frac {\Delta t} 2 \mathbf A ^ {-1} ( \mathbf f(\mathbf y^{t} ) + \mathbf f( \mathbf y^{t+1} ) ) $$
$$\begin{array}{rl} \kappa_1 &=& \mathbf A^{-1} \mathbf f( \mathbf y^t ) \\ \kappa_2 &=& \mathbf A^{-1} \mathbf f( \mathbf y^t + \Delta t \cdot \kappa_1 ) \\ \mathbf {y^{t+1}} - \mathbf {y^t} &=& \frac {\Delta t} 2 \mathbf ( \kappa_1 + \kappa_2 ) \\ \end{array} $$

RK4

$$\begin{array}{rl} \kappa_1 &=& \mathbf A^{-1} \mathbf f( \mathbf y^t ) \\ \kappa_2 &=& \mathbf A^{-1} \mathbf f( \mathbf y^t + \frac {\Delta t} 2 \cdot \kappa_1 ) \\ \kappa_3 &=& \mathbf A^{-1} \mathbf f( \mathbf y^t + \frac {\Delta t} 2 \cdot \kappa_2 ) \\ \kappa_4 &=& \mathbf A^{-1} \mathbf f( \mathbf y^t + \Delta t \cdot \kappa_3 ) \\ \mathbf {y^{t+1}} - \mathbf {y^t} &=& \frac {\Delta t} 6 (\kappa_1 + 2 \cdot \kappa_2 + 2 \cdot \kappa_3 + \kappa_4)\\ \end{array} $$

Backward Euler

$$\mathbf {y^{t+1}} - \mathbf {y^t} = \Delta t \mathbf A ^ {-1} \mathbf f( \mathbf y^{t+1} ) $$

assume $ \mathbf f( \mathbf y ) $ is linear, so $ \mathbf f( \mathbf y^{t+1}) = \mathbf B \mathbf y ^ {t+1} $, therefore

$$\begin{array}{rl} \mathbf {y^{t+1}} - \mathbf {y^t} &=& \Delta t \mathbf A ^ {-1} \mathbf B \mathbf y ^ {t+1} \\ (\mathbf I - \Delta t \mathbf A ^ {-1} \mathbf B) \mathbf y ^ {t + 1} &=& \mathbf y ^ t \end{array} $$

Symplectic Euler

$$\begin{array}{rl} \mathbf {\dot q}^{t+1} &=& \mathbf {\dot q}^t - \Delta t \frac k m \mathbf q^t \\ \mathbf {q}^{t+1} &=& \mathbf q^t + \Delta t \mathbf { \dot q} ^{t+1} \\ \end{array} $$

CSC417-Lecture-01

Lagrangian Mechanics

Newton’s Laws of Motion

  1. Every object will remain at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless compelled to change its state by the action of an external force.

as

  1. The force acting on an object is equal to the time rate-of-change of the momentum

momentum:

$$I = m\mathbf{v} $$

force

$$f = \frac{d}{dt}(m\mathbf{v}) = m\frac{d}{dt}(\mathbf{v}) $$
  1. For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction

Variational Mechanics (aka. Analytical Mechanics)

Kinetic Energy and Potential Energy

variational principle

$$e(f(t),\dot{f}(t),...) \Rightarrow \mathbb{R} $$

Generalized Coordinates

$$\begin{array}{ll} \mathbf{x}(t)&=\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{q}(t)) \\ \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt}(t)&=\frac{d\mathbf{f}}{d\mathbf{q}}\mathbf{\dot{q}(t)} \end{array} $$

example

$$\begin{array}{ll} \mathbf{x}(t) = R\mathbf{X} + \mathbf{p} \\ R \in SO_3 \\ \mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{R^3} \end{array} $$

The Lagrangian

$$\mathbf{L = T - V} \tag{1} $$

The Principle of Least Action (Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz)

Given the start point and the end point, find the path between them by finding a stationary point of the ACTION

$$S(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{\dot{q}}) = \int_{t_1}^{t_2}T(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{\dot{q}})- V(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{\dot{q}})dt $$

perturbation

$$S(\mathbf{q}+\delta\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{\dot{q}}+\delta\mathbf{\dot{q}}) = S(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{\dot{q}}) $$

The Calculus of Variations

$$\begin{array}{ll} S(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{\dot{q}}) &= \int_{t_1}^{t_2}L(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{\dot{q}})dt\\ S(\mathbf{q}+\delta\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{\dot{q}}+\delta\mathbf{\dot{q}}) &= \int_{t_1}^{t_2}L(\mathbf{q}+\delta\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{\dot{q}}+\delta\mathbf{\dot{q}})dt\\ &\approx \int_{t_1}^{t_2}L(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{\dot{q}})dt + \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{q}}\delta\mathbf{q} + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{\dot{q}}}\delta\mathbf{\dot{q}} dt \end{array} $$

with (2) & (3)

$$\begin{array}{ll} \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{q}}\delta\mathbf{q} + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{\dot{q}}}\delta\mathbf{\dot{q}} dt &= 0\\ \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{q}}\delta\mathbf{q} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{\dot{q}}}\delta\mathbf{q}dt + \frac{\partial L}{\partial\mathbf{\dot{q}}}\delta \mathbf{q}\big|_{t_0}^{t_1} &= 0 \end{array} $$

where

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial\mathbf{\dot{q}}}\delta \mathbf{q}\big|_{t_0}^{t_1} = 0 $$

Thus

$$\forall \delta \mathbf q \Rightarrow \int_{t_1}^{t_2} ( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{q}} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{\dot{q}}})\delta\mathbf{q}dt = 0 \\ $$

Euler-Lagrange Equation

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{q}} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{\dot{q}}} = 0 \tag{2} $$

Why do we care?

  1. It’s a unifying principle. Can derive equations of motion - Deformable Objects, Fluid, RigidBodies

Example: Mass-Spring Systems in 1D

Step 1: Configurations

$$\mathbf q = \mathbf x(t) \\ \mathbf {\dot q} = \mathbf v(t)\\ $$

Step 2: Kinetic Energy

$$T = \frac 1 2 m \mathbf v^2 $$

Step 3: Potential Energy

$$Hooke's Law:W = \int -k\mathbf x \mathbf v dt = \int -k\mathbf x d\mathbf x = -\frac 1 2 k \mathbf x^2 \\ $$
$$V = \frac 1 2 k \mathbf x^2 $$

Step 4: Lagrangian

$$L = T - V = \frac 1 2 m \mathbf{\dot q} ^ 2 - \frac 1 2 k \mathbf q^2 $$

Step 5: Euler-Lagrange Equation

$$\frac {\partial L} {\partial \mathbf q} = - k\mathbf q , \quad \frac {d} {dt} \frac {\partial L} {\partial \mathbf {\dot q}} = m\mathbf{\ddot q} $$
$$m\mathbf{\ddot q} = - k\mathbf q \tag 3 $$
